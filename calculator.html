🎓 Learn: Why does Position Sizing matter? (5 min read) ▼
Position sizing is the absolute secret of trading longevity. If you risk a fixed percentage (e.g. 1%) of your account, you would need to lose 100 trades in a row to wipe out your capital. But if you trade random sizes, a short streak of losses will deplete your account. Always adjust your share count based on how wide your stop loss is.

🎓 Learn: Why is 1:2 R:R the holy grail? (5 min read) ▼
If your risk-reward ratio is **1:2**, you only need to win **34%** of your trades to break even. If your ratio is **1:3**, you only need to win **25%** of your trades! This means you can be wrong most of the time and still grow your account. Poor risk-reward setups (like 1:0.5) require extremely high win rates (67%+) which are almost impossible to maintain.

🎓 Learn: What is ROI vs account gain? (5 min read) ▼
Return on Investment (ROI) calculates how much money you made relative to the amount you deployed. But it is different from your **Account Gain**. For instance, if you make a 10% ROI on a trade using 20% of your account size, your overall account grows by 2% (which is the actual growth). Good traders focus on account exposure relative to ROI.

🎓 Learn: What is Trading Expectancy? (5 min read) ▼
Expectancy is the mathematical edge of your system. If your expectancy is **+$50.00**, it means that even if you lose some trades, over a sample size of hundreds of trades you average a profit of $50.00 per trade. If your expectancy is negative, no amount of psychology or journaling will save your account; you must increase your average win size or win rate.
💡
Risk Tip: Never average down on a losing position. Cut losses quickly to protect capital.

❓ Quick Risk FAQs
What is the 2% rule? +
The 2% rule states you should never risk more than 2% of your account capital on a single trade. If you have a $10,000 account, your maximum loss should never exceed $200.
What is Slippage? +
Slippage occurs when your order is filled at a price different from the expected price (often due to high volatility). Keep stop losses wide enough to tolerate minor slippages.
What is expectancy? +
Expectancy is the average amount of money you expect to make (or lose) per trade over a large number of trades, taking win rates and size ratios into account.
